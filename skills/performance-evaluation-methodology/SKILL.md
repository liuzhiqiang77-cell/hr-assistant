# Performance Evaluation Methodology

## When to Use
- Reviewing performance where tangible output is not immediately visible
- Evaluating work with long-term impact (e.g., infrastructure, R&D)
- Rating managers whose organizations have poor performance
- Assessing performance where results lag behind activities

## Time Offset and Long-Term Assessment

### Step 1: Analyze Time Offset

Determine if output in the review period is linked to activities in the same period.

**Key Concept**: Current output may be the "light from distant stars"â€”reflecting work done months or years ago.

### Step 2: Judge Performance Based on Activities

| Situation | Evaluation Approach |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Tangible output absent, but activities strong | Give credit for performance-building activities |
| Building future capacity (e.g., setting up facility) | Do not penalize for lack of immediate output |
| Immediate outputs visible | Evaluate against standard metrics |

### Step 3: Weigh Long-Term vs. Short-Term

**Short-Term Outputs**:
- Meeting schedules
- Hitting quarterly targets
- Delivering immediate results

**Long-Term Activities**:
- Improving design methods
- Building infrastructure
- Developing systems and processes

### Step 4: Apply "Present Value" Concept

Estimate the current worth of future-oriented activities:
1. Calculate expected future payback over time
2. Determine what that future value is worth today
3. Include this "present value" in the current performance assessment

**Example**: An employee spending 6 months improving a manufacturing process may show no immediate output, but if the improvement saves $1M over 3 years, calculate and credit that present value in the current review.

## The Potential Trap Rule

### Step 1: Assess Substance Over Form

**Evaluate**:
- Actual performance and real output
- Tangible results delivered

**Ignore**:
- "Potential" defined as form (knowledge, appearance, "looking like a manager")
- Charisma or presentation skills without results

### Step 2: Apply the Organization Cap

**RULE**: The performance rating of a manager **cannot be higher** than the rating accorded to their organization.

| Organization Performance | Maximum Manager Rating |
|-------------------------|------------------------|
| Lost money | Cannot exceed "Poor" |
| Missed revenue forecasts | Cannot exceed "Below Expectations" |
| Slipped schedules | Cannot exceed "Meets Requirements" |
| Poor internal measures | Cannot exceed "Meets Requirements" |

### Step 3: Enforce Consequences

If the business unit:
- Lost money
- Missed revenue forecasts
- Slipped schedules
- Showed poor internal measures

**Then**: The manager **cannot receive a high rating**, regardless of personal traits or effort.

### Step 4: Prevent Negative Signaling

**Reject**: Excuses that "the organization failed but the manager is outstanding."

**Reinforce**: Performing like a manager is the requirement, not acting like one.

**Risk**: Allowing high ratings for managers of failing organizations signals that "acting" like a manager is sufficient.
