# Maslow Need Levels - Detailed Reference

## Physiological Needs

**Definition**: Basic necessities of life (food, clothing) that money can buy.

**Driver**: Fear of deprivation (e.g., fear of starvation).

**Example**: Working to avoid starvation (Dickens' era workers).

**Characteristics**:
- Most fundamental level
- Once satisfied, ceases to motivate
- Money viewed as utilitarian resource

## Safety/Security Needs

**Definition**: Desire to protect oneself from slipping back to deprivation.

**Driver**: Protection against catastrophic loss.

**Example**: Medical insurance protecting against bankruptcy from hospital fees.

**Characteristics**:
- Rarely dominant, but absence badly affects performance
- Focus on stability and predictability
- Money viewed as safety buffer

## Social/Affiliation Needs

**Definition**: Inherent desire to belong to a group with common traits.

**Driver**: Companionship and team identity.

**Example**: Taking a low-paying job solely for the companionship of colleagues.

**Characteristics**:
- "Misery loves not just any company, but the company of other miserable people"
- "Excited people want to be with excited people"
- Team identity becomes powerful motivator

## Esteem/Recognition Needs

**Definition**: Desire to keep up with or emulate a specific person or group ("keeping up with the Joneses").

**Driver**: Recognition from a specific reference group.

**Example**: An athlete greeting a top player feels great to the athlete, but means nothing to family.

**Characteristics**:
- Esteem exists in the eyes of the beholder
- Self-limiting: once goal reached, need loses urgency
- Money becomes scorecard for achievement

## Self-Actualization

**Definition**: Need to achieve one's "utter personal best" in a chosen field.

**Driver**: Growth and personal excellence.

**Characteristics**:
- Does not extinguish after fulfillment
- Drives performance to ever higher levels
- Money as measure of achievement, not need satisfaction
- Boundless motivation
