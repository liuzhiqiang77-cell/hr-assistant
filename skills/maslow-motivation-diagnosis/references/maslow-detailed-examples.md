# Detailed Maslow Need Level Examples

## Physiological Needs Examples

**Historical Context:**
- Dickens' era workers: "If I don't work, I starve"
- Clear, immediate consequence for not working

**Modern Context:**
- Entry-level workers living paycheck to paycheck
- Motivated primarily by base salary sufficient for basic expenses
- Once comfortable standard of living reached, this need stops motivating

## Security/Safety Needs Examples

**Corporate Benefits:**
- Health insurance as protection against medical bankruptcy
- Pension plans protecting against poverty in old age
- Job security protections (tenure, union contracts)

**Behavioral Indicators:**
- Employee reluctant to take risks even when potential reward is high
- Preference for stable, predictable compensation over variable pay
- Focus on benefits packages and protections

## Social/Affiliation Needs Examples

**"Misery loves company" phenomenon:**
- People gravitate toward others in similar emotional states
- Excited people seek excited colleagues
- Miserable people seek miserable colleagues

**Workplace manifestations:**
- Employee stays in department despite better offer elsewhere due to team bonds
- Social events and team building highly valued
- Workplace friendships are primary motivator

## Esteem/Recognition Needs Examples

**"Keeping up with the Joneses":**
- Focus on relative standing within reference group
- Promotion valued because it puts you at same level as respected peers

**Subjectivity example:**
- Young athlete greeted by star player → feels tremendous esteem
- Family members see same interaction → means nothing
- The "beholder" determines what confers esteem

**Self-limiting nature:**
- Goal: Become Vice President
- Once achieved → "Now what?" → motivation drops
- Need new esteem target to re-engage motivation

## Self-Actualization Examples

**Competence-Driven:**
- Musician practicing scales daily for years
- Teenager perfecting skateboard trick for hours
- Focus: Personal mastery, not comparison to others

**Achievement-Driven:**
- Entrepreneur constantly seeking bigger challenges
- Athlete always pushing to break personal records
- Focus: Testing limits of capability

**Peg/Ring Experiment Classification:**
- **Gamblers:** Choose impossible tasks (high risk, no control)
- **Conservatives:** Choose easy tasks (low risk)
- **Achievers:** Choose tasks at boundary of capability (50% success rate)
